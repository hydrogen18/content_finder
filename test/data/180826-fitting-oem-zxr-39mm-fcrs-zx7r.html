Fitting OEM ZXR 39mm FCRs to ZX7R
Some of you may have seen my other post asking for differences between 39 and 41mm flatslides (besides the obvious dimensions ;-P). Thanks for all the facts and advice. I have decided I would like to go with the 39s on my P model, even if it is not the norm. Of course this presents some challenges because the spacing is not the same, and not close enough to make small adjustments with. I am in the process of fitting a set of OEM 39s to my P model and thought I would share the process I am working through. Hopefully it will all work out in the end, but it is safe to say it is not going to be straight forward!

The carbs I bought were cheaper than usual, but they came with their own set of problems. Two of the velocity stacks are slightly broken and the carbs were absolutely filthy inside and out. These were the known issues I was made aware of when I bought them. What the seller did not tell me is that the emulsion tubes were missing and that he had changed the float bowls from short ones advertised in the picture to the long OEM ones that interfere with the race airboxes. I have complained about that and am waiting for the shorter ones to turn up. As for the emulsion tubes, CCM is going to try and source me some with a bit of luck!

So first things first. I had never held a set of FCRs in my hand, so time to start taking things apart and get an understanding of how it all fits together.



The solid element of the spacing all comes from these alloy tubes that fit between all the carbs. The two outside are hollow and the middle ones are threaded. An allen bolt goes through each side of the carb rack and pulls them all together tight against these tubes.



The bore spacing dimensions of the ZXR carbs which these came from are 75-75-75mm and the P model ZX7R carbs are 78-86-78mm. It doesn’t take a genius to work out how big these need to be. Each tube is 60mm long, so looking at the bore spacing dimension differences we can work out that the outside two need to be 3mm longer and the middle threaded one needs to be 11mm longer. So we need outside tubes at 63mm and inside threaded tubes at 71mm. There are some other parts that come into the equation, but let’s keep it simple and focus on these bits for now.

I bought some 8mm stainless tube with 1.5mm thick wall and cut the outside ones. I used a hack saw and cut them all slightly long and filled them down to the exact length. If you have special tools to get it right first time then go for it, but I know that no matter how hard I try I don’t always get a hack saw cut perfectly straight, which is why I took my approach with the file. I did the same with the inside tubes. I used 8mm again this time with a 2mm thick wall. Once cut to length I tapped out a thread for the original allen bolts. Now this was more painful than I thought, The inside diameter needs to be 4.5mm for the tap to start cutting, but the hole was 4mm. I had to drill them out with a 4.5mm drill bit. I had really good drill bits that just grabbed and would turn the tube around in the vice and cheap crap ones that didn’t cut at all. A lot of cheap drill bits broken… I finally got them all done. It would have been easier with an aluminium tube, or one that was actually 4.5mm to start with! I went for stainless because I was using the original bolts which because of the extending of the tubes, have 3mm less thread to grab. I wanted stainless so I could tighten properly over the risk of striping a soft metal.



There are other parts that had to be contended with. The tubes in which the accelerator pumps have their spring action on also act as a spacer. These look a little more complex to make new longer ones, so I just purchased some 8mm diameter, 3mm high stand off spacers. I did originally try to make my own spacers from the left of 8mm tube I had, but I could not manage to cut a perfectly parallel 3mm spacer with a hack saw, and it was hard to grip in the vice to file it down being so small.

You might be thinking this would have been easier instead of all the 63mm tubes I cut, but they have less of a recess to sit in and the 3mm spacer would push the original 60mm tubes out.



The other parts to contend with were the float bowl pressure tubes, the fuel pipe hook up and fuel line spacer. Lucky for me the float bowl pressure tubes (if that is what you call them) have rubber seals that move up and down the shaft, so they fit in nice. The other two were long enough not to be effected by the 3mm extension and dropped right in.



So, all bolted together with a bit of thread lock, just because of the less thread that the bolts were grabbing. Lined up beautiful to the spare engine I Just happen to have sitting on my garage floor.



Now that was the easy part. I have now got to make a new slide operating rod that works of the throttle (I am sure these have proper names lol). This will not be so easy, but I will update when I start on this next week :)

So I have made a little more progress on this....

I have made up a throttle shaft and it seems to be operating pretty well, although I have not run the carbs yet.

The original one is a solid 8mm stainless steel rod that runes through all 4 carbs at the top. The slides, accelerator pumps and throttle pull wheel all fix to it. I did originally try and make my own extended one from a stainless steel so it would be exactly like the original, but my drill bits just would not go through. I will probably revisit this with some better drills, but to get things moving I decided to make one from brass because is is easier to machine. Although more flexible and softer than steel, it should be strong enough for the job if you don't over-tighten the screws when you are done (like the previous owner of these seemed to do!).

So, here is how it is done. First off you need to remove the pull wheel. This is held on by a roll pin which I found a nail the correct width, cut the sharp end off and used it to punch the pin out by tapping it with a hammer. Picture below is not all that clear, because like an idiot I took it so that you cannot see the pin, but you get the idea.



You can by these pretty cheap if you put it in a safe place and forget where that place was like i did! It is also worth making a note of which way around the wheel goes now to save you scratching your head later.

The original stainless shorter shaft separated from the pull wheel you can see below next to the one I made.



Below is a image of how I went about drilling and tapping. The +mm indicates how much bigger the gap is compared to the original gap. These are the same increments I used to get the spacers right.

Red is throttle slide fixing holes. These had a slight wide countersink drilled into the top like the original and then tapped out with a M3.5 x 0.6 tap.

Green is accelerator pump fixing holes. These were drilled first with a 3.2mm drill bit, then aftwards drilled only half way with a 4mm drill bit. They were then tapped with a M4 x 0.7 tap so only the bottom half was threadded, like the originals.

white is the throttle pull cable roll pin fixing hole. This is a 2.5mm hole. It was measured by taking a figure from between it and the last throttle slide hole on the original. This figure will remain the same.



What is so important here is that every hole is lined up perfectly straight across the top and that every hole is drilled perfectly straight down and not at an angle. If you get this wrong you will have some slides opening and closing slightly different from each other because it will dictate the position of the slide pull arms. The accelerator pumps would also operate a different rates too although you might afford to be a smidge out on these. They are all important you get them right, but the most important of all is to ensure you get the roll pin hole spot on. If it does not go through perfectly straight down the pin will not go through and if it is not at the exact same angle as the throttle slide holes then you will likely end up with the throttles not opening up enough or not sitting fully closed. The picture above you can see I got this wrong because my small 2.5mm drill bit flexed when it went through and did not come through straight......it was the last hole I drilled after tapping out all the others and unfortunately it meant that the rod had to be done again! The one you see in the picture is not the one I finally used, but it illustrates what is required.

I used a pillar drill and a pillar drill vice to clamp it down. I stuck a strip of masking tape across the length of the rode to draw a straight line down and mark where to drill. Each time you take it out of the vice to drill the next hole you run the risk of not putting it back in exactly at the same rotation which could send your holes at different angles. Just make sure you really triple check each time and this can be done by eye, but it is not easy. There probably is a better more precise way depending on what tools you have to work with.

The reason I did not put an exact figure down for the accelerator pump holes is because they depend on where you put that 3mm spacer in my first post. The spacer tube where these operate from will be spaced 3mm to the left or the right. Make sure you take note when you put that spacer in and you just need to measure the original rod for the spacing between this hole and its left or right throttle slide hole. If you put the 3mm spacer on the right of the spacer tube then you just need to measure the original distance from the left throttle hole to the accelerator pump hole because it hasn't move on the left side. The opposite if you did it the other way. It sounds complicated, but if you have got this far it will make perfect sense.

After wasting my time with lots of thinking and coming up with ways to operate the choke, I thought I would try the standard CV carb choke operator and see if it could be modified. Much to my surprise and delight it goes straight on. It's a perfect fit! I won't be needing it as I am replacing the CV carbs with these.



Yes.... I am a screw missing ;)

I needed to come up with a way to attach the return spring. I just pinched one of the little coolant hose locators off the standard CV float bowl and used that as a bracket. The screw that holds it on the CV carbs is a perfect thread to go into the side of the FCR carb. See picture below for location:



All I need to do now is work out a way to hold the choke cable at the right distance from where it connects. The CV part is not a direct swap. Once that is done then it will be time to test these bad boys! The kit M carb kit that I got from CCM arrived yesterday so I have that all installed now too :)

Hi John,

The kit contained a selection of main jets, emulsion tubes, 2 sets of needles (FMK and FML) and the shorter red velocity stacks. It was the kit package for the M model 39mm carbs, so not entirely sure it is best suited to the air flow characteristics 41s.

The part number is 99997-1873, but you may struggle to find it for sale. If you search hard enough on the net you could probably piece most of it together. The emulsion tubes hole arrangement is different from stock, so I think they might have to come as part of the kit. I got my kit from CCM on here. He is pretty resourceful when it comes to parts. Might be worth asking him if there is a similar kit for the 41s? Just remember there will be the import duty to pay on goods from the States to the UK.
